Question: What do we conclude? What do we report?

Question: What is the specific interpretation in terms of the effect of defendant race?

On average, Black defendants are judged to be 8.5 units more guilty than White defendants, controlling for (holding constant) implicit bias.

Question: What does this model require with respect to this group comparison across various levels of implicit bias?

It requires that the magnitude of the Black defendant (vs. White defendant) group difference (\(b=8.5\)) be the same for all levels of implicit bias and the bias effect be the same for both defendant races.

Question: How can you remove that constraint?

Include an interaction between defendent race and bias.

Question: But what is now important to do with \(X\)s?

data <- data |> 
  mutate(black_defend_c = if_else(black_defend == 0, -.5, .5),
         bias_c = bias - mean(bias))

\(\text{guilt} = 43.9 + 8.5*\text{black defend}_c + 0.8*\text{bias}_c + .4*\text{black defend}_c*\text{bias}_c\)

Question: What is the interpretation of \(b= 0.8\) for bias (centered)?

This is the unweighted effect of bias averaged across White and Black defendants.

You can think about this like a main effect in ANOVA. For every one unit increase in bias, ratings of defendants’ guilt increased by 0.8 units when averaging across defendants of both races (holding constant race at its unweighted average), \(t(236)= 17.1, p < .001\).

You should also describe the effect with the 95% CI for \(b\) and a variance effect size estimate.

Question: How do we test for the interaction between Bias and Defendant Race on Guilt?

The test of the \(b\) for the product term (defendent race X bias) is the test of the interaction. It is significant, \(t(236)= 4.45, p< .001\).

Question: What does this mean to have an interaction and how do you interpret \(b = 0.4\)?

The magnitude of the effect of defendant race on guilt increases by 0.4 units for every one unit increase in bias.

The magnitude of the effect of bias is 0.4 units greater when the defendants are Black than when they are White.

11.4 Quantitative & Dichotomous: Dichotomous simple effects

Question: How do you test simple effects of defendant race at various levels of bias (and what levels might you choose to test)?

We want to report simple effects of defendant race at the mean of bias and one SD above and below mean (or other meaningful values).

Make sure the that the levels of bias at \(\pm1\) SD are represented in your data!

As we noted earlier, the coefficient for defendant race (8.5) from the centered model is the simple effect of defendant race at the mean of bias.

We need to perform two more transformations of bias to get the other simple effect tests for defendant race.

Centered model

\(\text{guilt} = 43.9 + 8.5*\text{black defend}_c + 0.8*\text{bias}_c + .4*\text{black defend}_c*\text{bias}_c\)

Bias is centered such that 0 = mean of bias.

We need to transform bias such that 0 = +1 SD of bias or -1 SD of bias.

It is counter-intuitive so be careful!

+1 SD = 0

Code 
data <- data |> 
  mutate(bias_hi = bias_c - sd(bias_c))

-1 SD = 0

Code 
data <- data |> 
  mutate(bias_lo = bias_c + sd(bias_c))

Note: It is easy to confuse these transformations. Do a logical check on your results.

m_bias_hi = lm(guilt ~ black_defend_c * bias_hi, data = data)

11.5 A sample brief report: Defendant race is focal

Defendant guilt ratings were analyzed in a General Linear Model with defendant race (Black vs. White) as a dichotomous variable and juror implicit bias (mean centered) as a quantitative variable. The interaction between these two variables was also included in the model.

The overall model accounted for a significant amount of variance in guilt ratings, \(R^2= 0.59, F(3,236) = 114.00, p < .001\). A significant main effect of defendant race was observed, 95% CI(\(b\))= [5.5,11.5], \(\Delta R^2=.06, t(236) = 5.66, p < .001\), such that Black defendants were rated as 8.5 units more guilty than White defendants among jurors of mean bias. However, juror bias significantly moderated the defendant race effect, 95% CI(\(b\))= [0.2,0.6], \(\Delta R^2=.03, t(236) = 4.45, p < .001\), indicating that the magnitude of the defendant race effect increased by 0.4 units for every one unit increase in juror bias (see Figure 1). Given this, the simple effect of defendant race was substantially reduced and non-significant among jurors who were low on bias (1 SD below the mean), 95% CI(\(b\))= [-2.4,5.6], \(\Delta R^2=.00, t(236)= 0.85, p= .399\). In contrast, the effect of defendant race was substantially larger and significant among jurors who were high on bias, 95% CI(\(b\))= [11.0,19.4], \(\Delta R^2=.09, t(236)= 7.15, p < .001\).
